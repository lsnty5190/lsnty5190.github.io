GemNet: Universal Directional Graph Neural Networks for Molecules

Summary

GemNet (NeurIPS 2021) is an extension of DimeNet [1] / DimeNet++ [2] for universally approximate equivariant functions. Compared with its prior work, GemNet leverages a two-hop message passing mechanism which incorporates more geometrical information as well as other beneficial techniques., i.e., geometric representation, symmetric message passing, and efficient bilinear layer. It shows outstanding performance on molecular dynamics prediction and obtains excellent results on COLL and MD17 datasets.

Main Review

The motivation of this article is to solve the expressive limitations of prior GNNs that rely on rotationally invariant predictions. The contributions that interest me are summarized as follows:

  • Prove of the sufficiency of $S^2$ sphere
    It gives the feasibility of using the spherical representation.
  • Design of two-hop message passing mechanism
    It introduces dihedral angles ($θ_{cabd}$ after rotation) compared with the prior works (directional message passing). Such design meets the integrity constraints of a molecular structure.
  • Employ some designs for training efficiency on message passing
    It would inspire us when designing the similar architecture based on message passing mechanism.

The author firstly elicits that the spherical representation on $S^2$ sphere is sufficient to approximate any rotationally equivariant function instead of using $SO(3)$ group [2], which lays the foundation for the subsequent geometric representation.

After proving the universality, the author then introduces the geometric message passing mechanism: (a) geometric representation; (b) symmetric message passing, and (c) efficient bilinear layer. (a) is the instantiated spherical representation and used for embedding geometrical information, i.e., distances, angles, and dihedral angles. (b) and (c) are techniques to improve the training efficiency.

The whole structure is shown in Fig. 1. It introduces some scaling factors to stabilize the activation variance. The final outputs of the model are the sum of the outputs embedding layer and several interaction layers.

Fig. 1 The GemNet architecture. T-MP/Q-MP denotes one/two-hop message passing

Discussion

Difference between one-hop and two-hop message passing?

Fig. 2 Geometrical information for atom $a$

As for the target atom $a$, its one-hop messages are the fusion of $x_{ba}$ , $x_{ca}$ , $\varphi_{cab}$ and $m_{ba}$ ; its two-hop messages are the fusion of its one-hop messages and $x_{db}$ , $\varphi_{abd}$ , $\theta_{cabd}$ and $m_{db}$ .

Are two-hop messages necessary?

Two-hop message passing introduces significant computational overhead. According to the experiment results, GemNet performs surprisingly well on MD17, but not on COLL. This suggests that one-hop message passing is expressive enough for some practical use cases, but two-hop message passing gives an advantage for the more challenging task of fitting multiple molecules at once. This part is further discussed in the next question.

Comparison of other geometrical information?

The author instantiates the spherical representation to geometric representation. However, it is equal to the spherical message introduced in [3] (illustrated in Fig. 3) if we fix atom c and treat the plane composed by atom a, b, and c as the reference plane. The dihedral angle between atom c and d is equal to the torsion angle of atom d. But the torsion angle can manifest the relative position of the one-hop atom, which the dihedral angle method might fail to capture.

Fig. 3 Spherical message passing

Compared with this modeling method, two-hop messages in GemNet additionally introduce the geometrical information of atom c’s structure information, i.e., $\varphi_{cab}$ and $x_{ca}$, and it leads to the computational overhead. But it also connects the different neighbor sets of atom a and atom b which would benefit the embedding updating and help the model to learn discriminative structure. These two structures can be further researched, for instance, can we introduce the torsion angle in two-hop messages of GemNet to directly contain the atom’s neighborhood structure?

Is geometrical information enough?

GemNet obtains excellent results on downstream predicting tasks. But it only focuses on geometric and neglects other information, e.g., atom type and bond type. Hence, can we design different learnable matrixes $W$ in terms of different type of atoms/bonds to help the model obtain heterogeneous results? Furthermore, another interesting research point is that can we reduce the filter and leverage the attention mechanism when designing the network? For instance, we can extend $W_{SBF}$ , $W_{CBF}$ and $W_{RBF}$ to attention matrixes and calculate the attention coefficients. These points can also be further researched.

Other assumptions

Can we adopt the Multiple Sequence Alignment (MSA) when extracting the features [5]? Should we add pooling layer before the final outputs (pooling the outputs of interaction layers)? Can we cluster the atoms after aggregation and leverage two neural networks to learn macro & micro information [6] / global & local information [7]? Should we focus more on expertise experience of molecular structure? Should we predict some intermediate attributes first and obtain the final prediction results, i.e., Forces and Energy when the direct solving is hard? Can we design some position embedding methods to better represent the molecular [8]?
